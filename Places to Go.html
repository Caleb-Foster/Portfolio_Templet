Top 10 Tech Destinations

What do geeks have, besides lying about having video game journalism credentials in order to get into E3? Waiting outside Skywalker Ranch until George Lucas feels you reaching out through the force and lets you inside for a romantic paddleboat ride around Lake Ewok? Hint: he probably won't. And on the off chance he does, he is going to make you do more than your share of the paddling.

As it turns out, we have quite a few options. We aren't just talking about well-trodden locales like the Smithsonian, either. Between awesome museums you probably haven't heard of, actual high-tech facilities, and shopping districts dedicated entirely to everything that is nerd, geek-friendly vacations are fun and easy to plan.

Whether you design your travel plans around a visit to one of these spots, or you want to convince your family to make a detour from Disneyworld, PCMag has combed the globe for the ten tech destinations you must visit before you die. This is, of course, if you are less than confident in your plan to cryogenically freeze your brain until the time that immortal and sexy robot bodies become feasible. Then you will have all the time in the world to go to the Kennedy Space Center and watch NASA send diplomatic chimp ambassadors to the Jupiter moon colonies.

1. The Computer History Museum - Mountain View, California

The world's largest museum dedicated to Information Age artifacts, the Computer History Museum is right at home in Silicon Valley. If you've ever wanted to do something sassy in front of the Google Street View car but have never seen one in the wild, now is your chance: the CHM has a dedicated exhibit featuring one of the actual cars in question. Other exhibits include The Babbage Engine, A History of Computer Chess (a.k.a. a missed opportunity for a living Battle Chess arena), and Internet History (you walk into a dark room and there is nary an artifact save for one illuminated marble bust of Al Gore).

2. The Tech Museum of Innovation - San Jose, California

Located in the heart of Silicon Valley, San Jose's premiere geek destination features cool exhibits like an earthquake simulator, a build-your-own-robot experience, and an infrared camera that will show everyone at the museum which parts of your body turn up red when seen through heat vision.

Act fast and you'll catch the $5 million Star Wars: Where Science Meets Imagination exhibit on the final leg of its tour. Hands-on Jedi Tech science workshops will help you build your own hovercrafts and lightsabers. Between the nearly 100 costumes and props and the interactive exhibits and design labs, no one have to force you to spend the day at this museum. Sorry, I'll see myself out.

3. Living Computer Museum - Seattle, Washington

Sadly, this is not the place where Skynet documents the events leading up to its becoming self-aware on August 29, 1997. It is still pretty cool, though. Visitors to Seattle should definitely stop by the Living Computer Museum, curated by none other than Microsoft cofounder Paul G. Allen. The LCM's collection includes vintage computers that you can actually interact with. We know you've always wanted to know what using a Teletype was really like. If you enjoy your visit, try Allen's two other Seattle area museums. The Flying Heritage Collection showcases his collection of airplanes, and the Experience Music Project features music, sci-fi, and other pop culture memorabilia. Currently, the EMP is offering a horror exhibit curated by directors Eli Roth, John Landis, and Roger Corman. No word on whether the entire space is filled with props from Landis's Blues Brothers 2000.

4. Boeing Factory - Everett, Washington

The Boeing Factory in Everett, Washington, is the world's largest building by volume. The 98.3 acre building is so vast, it has its own fire department, medical clinic, and water treatment plant. You could not cause an incident there even if you tried. Which you shouldn't, because they have their own security force, too.

a 472 million cubic foot building is cool enough, but when you get to watch 747's being assembled? That's worth a special trip. The Future of Flight aviation center features plenty of interactive exhibits designed to teach you about aircraft design and aviation. You'll then get to take a tour through the actual facility for a behind-the-scenes look that is so legit you aren't even allowed to take pictures.

5. The Centre for Computing History - England

Even though England spells “center” wrong, you can still learn something from this collection of vintage computers, game consoles, and calculators. Most of the computers and consoles are up and running. If you've ever wanted to play on a vintage Amiga, BBC Micro, or Sinclair ZX, this is one of the only chances you will probably get. You can even check out some newer models, like the Raspberry Pi. The Centre also explores the business and legal aspects of the Information Age.

If you are interested in interacting on an overnight basis with other people who like vintage computers (we can't promise this is going to go well), the museum also offers cool events like a retro gaming lock-in where you can play on a bunch of old systems from the original Nintendo Entertainment System to the system that the Zelda franchise would like to forget, the Philips CD-I.

6. The Intel Museum - Santa Clara, California

You probably won't be able to meet the adorable hurt-feelings robot from those old Intel commercials, but a visit to the Intel Museum is still worth it. Located inside Intel's headquarters in Santa Clara, California, the building awash with blue and gold is what I imagine it would be like to live in the magical land of Sandy Bridge.

7. Akihabara District - Tokyo, Japan

Look. You call a store “Super Potato,” and I'm going to spend all my money there regardless of what you sell. When what you sell is video games, though, I'm not only going to spend all my money, I'm going to spend the money of everyone I know. Vintage and rare games galore line the shelves of this retro gaming palace. I'm pretty sure they have stuff for sale there that isn't even on the internet.

That's only one shop to visit in Tokyo's Akihabara District, the epicenter of otaku and gaming culture in Japan. You can also spend your time being the big American creep at one of the maid cafes, which are exactly what they sound like: offensive.

8. Kennedy Space Center - Cape Canaveral, Florida

NASA could be defunded at any time. At least it feels like that, what with space travel becoming the purview of for-profit entities like Virgin Galactic. Manned space flights aren't happening anytime soon, but you should still be able to catch an unmanned rocket launch. Before Richard Branson ruins it for everyone who can't afford a $250,000 ticket to go to suborbital “space” with Justin Bieber, you should head to Florida and do star-hunk Neil deGrasse Tyson proud.

Visitors to the Kennedy Space Center can also check out the visitor complex, which boasts a variety of exhibits and artifacts related to the coolest government program ever.

9. International Spy Museum - Washington DC

Want to see a bunch of cool declassified CIA technology? So do a bunch of hysterical eight year-olds. Head to the International Spy Museum during off hours so you can get up close and personal with cool vintage tech, like enigma machines, lipstick pistols, and the CIA's rectal tool kit (a kit of escape tools you store in your rectum, not a kit full of tools to be used on people's rectums). Visitors can also check out a James Bond exhibit (including an actual Bond car) or use a GPS to hunt down clues around the city and unlock a secret passcode. If Julia Child got to be both a spy and a beloved chef (this is actually true), you should at least be able to be a pretend spy.

10. CERN - Geneva Switzerland

If you want to build a time-traveling superhuman army that is also sexy to look at, you're going to want to be somewhere where you can both a.) take advantage of a national policy of political neutrality and b.) trick Nordic models into entering a black hole by leaving a trail of sumptuous chocolates. Enter Geneva, Switzerland. Visitors are welcome at CERN, the world's largest particle physics laboratory.
